State-of-the-art 

Many functions in modern vehicles, such as autonomous driving, driver assistance systems, and low-level motion control, require accurate information about the vehicle’s states [Singh, Rodríguez, Morrison]. Therefore, precise knowledge of vehicle states and parameters plays a key role in facilitating effective and efficient operation of ground vehicles. While there is a solid body of literature regarding state and parameter estimation for single-unit vehicles [Ghorai], there have been remarkably few attempts to tackle vehicle-trailer systems [Korayem]. Heavy-duty vehicles present unique architectural and operational challenges that render standard automotive techniques insufficient.  

Current solutions mainly rely on proprioceptive sensors, such as wheel speed sensors and IMUs, and can deliver accurate speed estimates under many conditions. However, these systems have well-documented limitations due to various factors. Firstly, low-friction conditions can cause excessive wheel slip, which significantly degrades the estimation performance using data from wheel speed sensors. Additionally, uncertainty in parameters, e.g., varying tire radius due to changes in load and pressure, introduces additional errors into converting the wheel’s rotational speed into translational speed, which in turn degrades the state estimates. Lastly, while the inertial measurements from an IMU can be integrated to enhance the estimates, the inherent drift over time in this approach prevents it from fully resolving the issues.   

Consequently, there is a growing interest in developing new technologies to address the existing shortcomings. In this regard, exteroceptive sensors, such as camera, radar, and lidar, provide significant complementary information as external aids to traditional methods. Due to their widespread presence in production vehicles and their robustness against environmental conditions, the project primarily focuses on the utilization of automotive radars. The existing approaches can be divided into two main categories. The first group of studies considers the use of conventional automotive radars, which typically produce a point cloud at each measurement scan, containing the positions of the return points and, potentially, the Doppler velocity for each measurement. Some studies process point cloud data from multiple time instants, performing point-to-point or feature matching to determine the displacement of the ego-vehicle, which subsequently enables the computation of ego-motion, [Park, Michaelis, Zhuang]. While this approach leads to satisfactory motion estimation, its performance might deteriorate in environments with sparse salient features, for example, in wide-open spaces, such as long tunnels or motorways, making it difficult to ensure reliable performance under all operating conditions. Additionally, the high computational complexity might hinder adoption on automotive platforms with limited processing capabilities. Other methods directly exploit Doppler velocity measurements available from a single measurement scan to estimate the ego-motion, [Kellner, Doer]. To this end, the measurements returned from the stationary objects are identified by the Random Sample Consensus (RANSAC) algorithm, which is known to be vulnerable when the received data is dominated by the dynamic elements in the surrounding. Another branch of the literature focuses on the use of ground speed radars that are directed to observe the road surface. Consequently, the resulting Doppler velocity measurements are guaranteed to be due the ego-motion exclusively. Such radars have been studied across various mobile platforms, including railway vehicles, offroad vehicles, agricultural machinery, construction equipment, passenger cars, and trucks. The emphasis has primarily been on hardware development and the effective processing of the radar backscatter signals, [Shariff, Reissland, Albrecht].   

Alternatively, lidar sensors, which are prevalent in autonomous vehicles, have recently begun to be deployed in production vehicles. Producing rich point cloud data that capture a detailed representation of the environment, these sensors’ outputs can be processed by similar techniques, as used for radars, to obtain motion estimates [Talbot, Lee]. In particular, Frequency-Modulated Continuous Wave (FMCW) lidar is an emerging technology [Behroozpour, Royo, Wu] that offers a promising solution also in environments with geometric degeneracy. Like conventional time-of-flight lidars, FMCW lidar can generate dense point clouds. Besides, it provides Doppler velocity measurements for each detected point, offering valuable information about instantaneous ego-motion. 

Despite this progress, three states that are central to heavy-vehicle safety and control remain insufficiently addressed: lateral velocity, articulation angle, and coupling force. These states directly determine stability margins, path-following quality, and the risk of dangerous events such as jackknifing. An estimation method that is nominally accurate but poorly calibrated in its uncertainty is actively harmful in safety-critical operation, since overconfident estimates compress decision margins without warning.

Results from our previous work on force-based modeling for articulated heavy vehicles [CITE] show that these three states can be estimated with practically useful accuracy, and that the complexity of articulated-system models can be managed through suitable decoupling strategies. However, articulation-angle accuracy is not yet sufficient for high-precision trailer-position reconstruction in demanding maneuvers, such as reversing. Additionally, performance deteriorates at low speed and under low-friction conditions, and remains sensitive to upstream measurement assumptions — in particular, uncertainty in effective wheel radius propagates directly into all velocity estimates.

Taken together, these results establish a foundation for the present project while also exposing its key open challenges: a system-level estimation framework for the full vehicle combination that fuses our model-based approach with exteroceptive sensing. Without this, reliable operation across the full range of real-world conditions — varying payloads, road surfaces, and driving scenarios — cannot be guaranteed.
